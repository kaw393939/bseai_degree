<p class="eyebrow">BSEAI Pitch · April 2026 · Keith Williams</p>

## The One-Sentence Claim

The B.S. in Enterprise AI is a studio-based, enterprise-facing degree for the
people who will design, operate, integrate, and govern AI-enabled systems in
real organizations.

## What This Presentation Asks You To Decide

The central question is whether NJIT should launch a B.S. in Enterprise AI as a
serious institutional response to where technical work is moving.

The answer depends on three judgments.

- Is enterprise AI implementation now a distinct labor-market need?
- Does the proposed curriculum prepare students for that layer of work?
- Does NJIT have the institutional base to do this credibly?

## Continuity, Not Rebrand

Evolving the WIS program into the Enterprise AI degree — a natural progression
driven by my research work and industry projects using Generative AI to train,
augment, and automate software engineering and product development processes.

The [author-context handout](/author-context) makes this argument with
primary-source detail: twenty-three years at NJIT, more than 10,000 students,
the WIS program founded in 2002 evolving into BSEAI in Fall 2026.

## The Degree Claim

The stronger claim is that organizations need graduates who can turn model
capability into reliable systems, governed workflows, and real institutional
value. That means the degree has to be judged on deployment, integration,
evaluation, governance, communication, and enterprise context, not on
frontier-model hype.

## The Evidence Paths

The handouts linked from the presentation follow the main proof paths behind
that claim.

- historical change and the broader AI transition
- benchmark and capability acceleration
- enterprise deployment bottlenecks
- emerging role clusters and labor demand
- governance and responsible deployment
- curriculum progression and graduate formation

Every number and quotation on this site traces to an entry in the sources

## What A Serious Reading Should Look For

The more important question is whether the argument connects clearly from
labor-market need to curriculum structure to student capability to
institutional credibility. If those links hold, the degree becomes much easier
to defend.
