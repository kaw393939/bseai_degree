## Seven undergraduate AI programs. One enterprise-facing seat.

The B.S. in Enterprise AI is a studio-based, enterprise-facing degree for
the people who will design, operate, integrate, and govern AI-enabled
systems in real organizations. This handout shows who else is teaching an
undergraduate AI degree, what makes BSEAI distinct, and what the
labor-market numbers say about the degree it will compete with.

## Strategic Conclusion

The peer set is becoming more crowded. Multiple institutions in the region
and beyond now offer undergraduate AI degrees, but most are centered on
computer science, mathematics, algorithms, and traditional AI subfields.
That makes the positioning problem sharper — but it also creates whitespace.
BSEAI does not try to out-CS the CS-heavy degrees. It claims the territory
of **enterprise implementation, agentic workflow design, data integration,
UX, systems analysis, productization, and responsible deployment**.

The strongest one-sentence positioning this handout supports is:

> The B.S. in Enterprise AI prepares students to design, deploy, evaluate,
> and operate enterprise-grade agentic systems at the intersection of
> software, data, workflows, and business value.

## The Labor-Market Frame BSEAI Is Built For

- **WEF Future of Jobs 2025:** **170M** new roles created, **92M**
  displaced, **78M** net gain by 2030. **63%** of employers name skills
  gaps as the top barrier to transformation.
- **LinkedIn Labor Market Report (Jan 2026):** AI-literacy job listings up
  **6×+** year-over-year; **1.3M+** AI-related opportunities 2023–2025;
  Forward-Deployed Engineer roles up **~42×** since 2023.
- **PwC AI Jobs Barometer 2025:** **56%** AI-skill wage premium; skills in
  most-AI-exposed jobs changing **66%** faster than in less-exposed jobs.

*Read this as recomposition, not elimination.* The fastest-growing skills
are AI and big data, cybersecurity, and technological literacy. The binding
constraint is the skills gap, not the job count.

## Realistic Optimism — The Copy Discipline We Apply Across The Site

Enterprise AI productivity has micro-level evidence (Brynjolfsson et al.
**14%** customer-support gain; Noy & Zhang writing tasks) and large macro
estimates (McKinsey **$2.6T–$4.4T** annual value; Goldman Sachs **~7%**
GDP over a decade). It does not yet have broad enterprise-wide aggregate
gains. The credible 2025–2035 story is rapid capability growth, uneven
diffusion, significant complementarity needs, and lumpy enterprise impact.
BSEAI is the degree for the decade of redesign and reskilling that this
evidence predicts.

The **productivity J-curve** (Brynjolfsson, Rock, Syverson) is the site's
canonical explanation for why aggregate gains arrive slowly even when
capability is rising fast: general-purpose technologies require years of
complementary investment in workflows, skills, and organizational design
before aggregate productivity gains become visible. Everywhere else on the
site that wants to hedge a macro claim links back to this paragraph.

Grace et al. 2023 expert survey of 2,778 AI researchers places the median
50% HLMI estimate at **2047** (published as an expert survey, not a
forecast; this site treats AGI/HLMI timelines as scenario language, never
as asserted fact).
