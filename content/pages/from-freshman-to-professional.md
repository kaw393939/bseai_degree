## An integrated studio architecture, not a course list

The degree should be understood not as a loose collection of AI courses, but
as an integrated studio architecture across systems, data, design, analytics,
requirements, and enterprise infrastructure.

## A Four-Year Build Path

Students do not enter college ready to architect enterprise AI systems. They
grow into that responsibility through a sequence that starts with software
discipline, moves through systems integration, and ends in deployment and
evaluation work.

## Year 1: Foundations

In the first year, students build the habits that make later technical work
possible.

- programming and debugging
- interface construction
- structured problem solving
- data fundamentals
- technical communication

By the end of this stage, students should be able to read errors, explain
their decisions, and produce work that other people can trust. That
trustworthiness is the beginning of all later AI work.

## Year 2: Integration

Separate technical skills start turning into systems thinking.

- requirements and stakeholder framing
- workflows rather than isolated assignments
- team delivery and iteration
- systems thinking instead of single-task completion
- chunking larger work into units they can reason about and test

This is the stage where students start acting less like assignment completers
and more like builders.

## Year 3: Applied AI And Enterprise Context

Once the software and systems base is real, the curriculum becomes clearly
AI-inflected.

- retrieval and search
- model behavior and evaluation
- workflow automation
- data-centric system design
- governance and risk awareness
- context management across longer-running systems

Product engineers, forward-deployed engineers, and AI systems integrators
begin to emerge here.

## Year 4: Deployment And Capstone

The late-program goal is not frontier-model research. It is the ability to
inspect, modify, evaluate, explain, and deploy complex systems that combine
architecture, interfaces, retrieval, orchestration, testing, stakeholder
reasoning, and runtime checks.

Students also learn when a system is drifting, overclaiming, or not yet ready
to ship — the judgment documented in
[Managing Drift and Runtime Integrity](/managing-drift-and-runtime-integrity).
