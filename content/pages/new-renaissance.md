## A 300-year unfolding, not a single year

The first Renaissance did not happen in a week. It took roughly 300 years to
unfold — a long, uneven re-composition of what it meant to make, learn,
govern, and trade. This handout uses that pacing frame deliberately. It
rejects technological determinism in both directions — neither a
near-certain utopia nor a near-certain collapse — and adopts instead the
discipline of **realistic optimism**: rapid capability growth, uneven
diffusion, significant complementarity needs, and lumpy enterprise impact.

## Exponential Progress (And The Caveat)

The visible numbers are real.

- ChatGPT reached **100 million** monthly active users in two months, the
  fastest consumer technology adoption ever recorded.
- FDA AI/ML-enabled device authorizations rose from **6** in 2015 to over
  **223** in 2023.
- Waymo is running **~150,000 driverless rides per week** in multiple cities
  as of 2026.
- Enterprise AI use rose from **~55%** of organizations in 2023 to **~88%**
  by 2025.

The caveat must be stated immediately, verbatim from the author's essay:

> Most business AI use remains superficial — employees using ChatGPT for
> drafting emails, teams experimenting with copilots, pilots that never reach
> production. Adoption breadth is not the same as institutional depth.

Every number on this page must be read alongside its paired scale-gap figure.
See [Enterprise Deployment Gap](/enterprise-deployment-gap) for the full
table.

## The Calculator Analogy (Verbatim)

> The calculator did not end mathematics education; it changed what
> mathematics education needed to teach. The goal stopped being mechanical
> arithmetic and started being the judgment of when, why, and how to apply
> mathematical reasoning. The same pattern is now unfolding in cognitive
> work more generally. AI does not end the need for judgment, framing,
> evaluation, and accountability — it raises the premium on them.

## Why Open With Fifth Avenue?

The 1900 and 1913 photos are not decorative. They compress the core claim
into one visual pattern: the machine changes first, then the street, the
labor model, the supply chain, and the institution reorganize around it.

In 1900, the street made sense for horses. By 1913, the street made sense for
cars. The important fact is not only that the vehicles changed — it is that
the surrounding world had to reorganize itself around the new substrate.

## Why This Matters For AI

AI is producing a similar transition in cognitive labor. The first visible
layer is easy to notice — chat interfaces, copilots, workflow tools, model
APIs. The deeper transformation happens underneath: requirements change,
apprenticeship models change, quality assurance changes, organizational
workflows change, labor demand shifts toward integration and evaluation.

The future will not belong only to those who can generate outputs with AI.
It will belong to those who can frame the problem, evaluate the output,
integrate the system, manage the process, communicate the result, and take
responsibility for the decision.

## 2035 Scenarios — Three Framings, None Of Them Inevitable

The author's essay on the second renaissance proposes three explicit
scenarios rather than a single forecast. This is the
site's discipline for every long-horizon claim.

### Optimistic — Broad Diffusion, Strong Complementarity

- Most enterprise work is meaningfully AI-augmented by 2035.
- The 20th-century productivity J-curve lag is shorter than expected.
- Labor recomposition is substantial; education systems adapt in time.

### Realistic Middle — Lumpy Diffusion, J-Curve Holds

- Micro-level productivity gains stay strong; macro-aggregate gains arrive
  slowly and unevenly.
- Some industries (software, customer support, legal research) are
  transformed; others remain largely unchanged.
- The binding constraint is skills, complementary investment, and
  organizational design.

### Challenging — Governance Shock, Capability Plateau, Or Both

- A major AI incident triggers regulatory retrenchment.
- Or: capability plateaus despite continued scaling; enterprise returns
  disappoint.
- Or both, in which case the deployment layer still matters — in fact,
  matters more.

In all three scenarios, the graduate profile BSEAI is designed for — the
person who can integrate, evaluate, govern, and operate AI-enabled systems —
is the one the economy needs.

## The Most Important Questions Aren't Technical (Verbatim)

> 1. How do we distribute the gains of AI broadly rather than narrowly?
> 2. How do we preserve human agency, judgment, and accountability when
>    machines can act on our behalf?
> 3. How do we educate the next generation to be the authors of this
>    transition rather than its passive subjects?
> 4. How do we build institutions — governance, education, labor,
>    information — that can adapt at the pace this transition requires?

## A Renaissance, Not A Revolution (Verbatim)

> The first Renaissance emerged gradually from medieval society, with fits
> and starts, regional variations, and centuries of adaptation. It produced
> extraordinary good — and also extraordinary harm — and the outcome was
> not preordained. It depended on the choices of institutions, cities,
> universities, printers, artists, and governments.
>
> Our Second Renaissance will likely follow a similar arc — substantial
> positive change over time, but achieved through hard work, wise
> governance, and human agency, not through technological determinism or
> overnight transformation.

## The Educational Consequence

If the machine layer changes and the institution does not, the institution
becomes misaligned with the world it claims to prepare students for. The
strongest argument for the B.S. in Enterprise AI is therefore not trend
following — it is institutional adaptation. The degree is meant to prepare
students for the 1913 world, not nostalgically optimize them for 1900.
