## Designed for the students who will actually enroll

The degree is designed for the students who will actually enroll. That means
closing documented gaps in college readiness, AI literacy, and professional
habits through the studio spine — not assuming they have already been closed.

The site's copy rule is explicit: we do not use generational-stereotype framing. We say
**incoming students arrive with uneven readiness**, and we cite the data.

## What The Data Actually Shows

- **NAEP Grade 12 Reading (2024):** proficiency rates remain well below
  pre-pandemic levels and have not recovered.
- **ACT 2024 Graduating Class National Report:** composite scores at
  multi-decade lows; STEM-benchmark readiness similarly depressed.
- **NCES School Pulse (2024–25):** chronic absenteeism well above
  pre-pandemic baselines across grade levels.
- **RAND K–12 AI Instruction (RRA4180-1):** structured AI instruction is
  reaching only a minority of K–12 students as of 2025.
- **RAND K–12 AI Policy (RRA956-31):** state-level policy guidance remains
  fragmented; many districts have no published AI-use policy.
- **NACE Job Outlook 2025 / 2026:** employers rank *communication,
  teamwork, problem-solving, and professionalism* above technical skills
  when describing what early-career graduates most need.

Read together, the evidence is narrow and specific. It is not a generational
indictment. It is a description of the on-ramp BSEAI graduates will walk
onto — and the formation work the degree has to do on purpose.

## The Studio-Formation Table

Eight rows — one per studio surface — specifying what incoming gap the
course addresses and what evidence of formation the student produces.

| Studio surface | Incoming gap closed | Formation evidence the student produces |
|---|---|---|
| IS 117 — Intro to Website Development | Concrete-system literacy; running code; basic HTML/CSS/JS fluency | A working personal site, deployed; a peer-reviewed code artifact |
| IS 218 — Building Web Applications | Requirements → running software; debugging; version control | A small multi-page app with a backend; a pull-request trail |
| IS 265 — Information Systems | Business context; stakeholder vocabulary; systems thinking | A systems-analysis memo + interview notes |
| IS 331 — Database Design | Data modeling; transactional vs analytical thinking | A normalized schema + query pack against a realistic dataset |
| IS 390 — Requirements Analysis & Systems Design | Scope discipline; spec writing; acceptance criteria | A written specification + sprint plan + invariants list |
| IS 392 — AI-Driven Text Analytics | Evaluation literacy; prompt/spec discipline; truthfulness | An evals pack with scored transcripts + a failure analysis |
| IS 425 — Enterprise AI Applications & Infrastructure | Deployment; orchestration; release evidence | A governed agentic workflow + release notes + monitoring plan |
| IS 465 / IS 480 — Analytics & Data-Centric AI | Data-centric judgment; retrieval and evaluation; productization | A data pipeline + retrieval system + evaluation report |
| IS 491 / IT 491 — Capstone | Integration across the above; stakeholder delivery | A published capstone with acceptance criteria met and evidence attached |

Each row describes an **artifact** the student produces — not a topic the
student is exposed to. Formation is measured in evidence, not coverage.

## Why This Belongs In The Curriculum Argument

Employers rank communication, problem-solving, teamwork, and professionalism
above technical skills. A degree that cannot demonstrate formation along
those axes does not produce the graduate employers are asking for — no
matter how technical the catalog description looks.

The studio spine makes formation the medium of instruction. Students write
specs, defend acceptance criteria, run verification, and produce release
evidence repeatedly, across four years, in increasingly consequential
artifacts. That is what "whole-student formation" means on this site.
